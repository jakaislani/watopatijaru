Lang Lang | Lifestyle and Vacations

Lifestyle

Multi-millionaire pianist Lang Lang lives a luxurious lifestyle with a net worth of $30 million.

The pianist started his career in a Beijing slum under a brutal practice regimen. Today, he lives in New York and leads a rock star lifestyle.

The young prodigy became his parents’ only priority when he was born in China during the one-child policy.

His father quit his job as a police officer and moved his mother and son to Beijing, where he planned to enroll Lang in the famed Central Conservatory of Music.

However, Lang was kicked out of his piano tutor’s studio when he was nine due to a lack of talent.

Strangely, when his father learned the news, he told the kid to commit suicide.

Nonetheless, they have a normal relationship now, and his father stays at home to manage his business in China.

He has now surpassed his father’s goal and has played in every major city worldwide.

Although with a flourishing profession, Lang injured his arm and suffered a setback.

Sadly, his arm problem worsened when he was diagnosed with tendonitis in 2017, threatening to destroy his career.

Due to their extensive playing, many pianists experienced the disease, but fortunately, Lang recovered quickly.

Lang married German Korean pianist Gina Alice Redlinger in June 2019 in a flashy ceremony in France.

Their marriage ended months of rumors about their secretive engagement. In January 2021, Gina gave birth to their first child.

Lang Lang | Career

Lang performed as a featured soloist at the opening concert of the China National Symphony at 14.

Similarly, he was the first pianist from China to perform for the Berlin and Vienna Philharmonics.

Lang has received attention from critics and musicians worldwide.

He is frequently praised for his ability to bridge the gap between his roles as a classical prodigy and a rock-like superstar.

When Lang burst onto the classical music community in 1999, his performance approach was controversial.

Although he has been criticized for being immature, others have acknowledged that he is a brilliant classical musician who can win crowds with youthful energy.

Following a sold-out Carnegie Hall debut, he flew to Beijing in 2001 as part of a tour honoring the Philadelphia Orchestra’s 100th anniversary.

There, he played for an 8,000-person crowd at the Great Hall of the People. He debuted at the BBC Proms that same year.
